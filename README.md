# Telco Customer Retention & Churn Analysis

## Project Overview

This project analyses customer churn behaviour for a telecommunications company using a real-world dataset of 7,043 customers. 
The dashboard was built in Microsoft Power BI and focuses on identifying why customers leave, which segments are most at risk, 
and what the business can do to improve retention.

**Tool:** Microsoft Power BI Desktop  
**Dataset:** Telco Customer Churn (IBM Sample Dataset)  
**Records:** 7,043 customers  

## Dashboard KPIs

Metric Values: 
Total Customers: 7,043
Churn Rate %: 26.54%
Churned Customers: ~2,000
Revenue Lost: $2.86M 


Key Insights

1. Churn rate is critically high
At 26.54%, more than 1 in 4 customers is leaving. The industry benchmark for telecom churn is typically 1.5–2% per month. This level of churn represents a serious revenue and growth problem that requires immediate strategic attention.

2. Contract type is the single biggest churn driver
Month-to-month customers churn at dramatically higher rates than one-year or two-year contract customers. Customers on flexible contracts have no switching barrier — they can leave at any time with no penalty. This is the most actionable finding in the entire dataset.

3. Fiber optic customers churn the most
Despite being a premium, higher-priced service, fiber optic customers account for the largest share of churned customers at 61.38% of all churns. This strongly suggests a perceived value or service quality problem — customers are paying more but not feeling they receive enough in return.

4. Electronic check payment is a high-risk signal
Customers who pay by electronic check churn at nearly 90%, far above any other payment method. This segment likely overlaps with month-to-month, low-commitment customers and should be treated as a priority retention target.

5. Gender does not drive churn
Male and female customers churn at near-identical rates across all payment methods. Gender is not a useful segmentation variable for churn prediction and can be deprioritised in retention campaigns.

6. Add-on services act as loyalty anchors
Customers without Online Security and Tech Support services churn significantly more than those who have them. These services increase switching cost — customers who are embedded in the product ecosystem are harder to lose.

7. New customers are most vulnerable
Churn is highest in the first 12 months of a customer's life. Customers who survive past two years churn at very low rates, suggesting that if the business can get customers past the early period, they become long-term loyal accounts.


Retention Drivers

Based on the dashboard analysis, the following factors correlate strongly with customers who stay:

- Longer contract commitments (one year or two year)
- Automatic payment methods (bank transfer or credit card)
- Subscription to add-on services (Online Security, Tech Support, Device Protection)
- Higher tenure (2+ years with the company)
- Having a partner or dependents

Customer Lifetime Trends

- Customers with longer tenure generate significantly higher total charges
- Month-to-month customers represent a large volume of transactions but low lifetime value due to high churn
- Two-year contract customers have the highest average total charges, confirming that retention directly drives revenue
- The first 0–12 months is the highest-risk window for churn — this is when the business must invest most in onboarding and engagement


Recommendations

 1. Convert month-to-month customers to annual contracts 
Offer a meaningful incentive — 10–15% monthly discount or a free add-on service — for month-to-month customers who upgrade to a one-year contract.
This single action has the highest potential return of any retention initiative in this dataset.

3. Investigate and fix the fiber optic experience
Fiber optic customers are your most expensive to acquire and your most likely to leave. Conduct a targeted NPS or satisfaction survey with this segment immediately.
If the product is under-delivering on speed or reliability, fix it. If it is a pricing perception issue, introduce a loyalty pricing tier.

3. Build an aggressive first-year onboarding programme
Since churn peaks in the first 12 months, create a structured onboarding journey — proactive check-in calls at 30, 90, and 180 days, a welcome discount, and guided adoption of add-on services. Every customer who reaches month 13 is significantly more likely to stay for years.

4. Target electronic check customers with payment method migration
These customers churn at nearly 90%. Run a campaign offering a small monthly bill credit to switch to automatic bank transfer or credit card. 
Automatic payment removes friction, reduces missed payments, and is strongly correlated with retention.

5. Bundle add-on services into base plans
Online Security, Tech Support, and Device Protection are your hidden retention tools. Customers who have them stay longer.
Consider including one or two of these in base plans at no extra cost — the retention value far outweighs the revenue sacrifice.

6. Introduce a senior citizen loyalty programme
Senior citizens are a vulnerable segment. A simplified billing process, dedicated support line, and a modest senior loyalty discount would reduce churn in this group
and build brand goodwill.

7. Offer tenure-based loyalty rewards
Customers who have been with the company for 2, 3, or 5 years should receive visible, meaningful recognition — a loyalty discount, a free upgrade, or priority support. This gives customers a reason to stay and a milestone to work toward.
